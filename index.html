Blood Typing Protocol

Blood typing determines which ABO group and Rh factor a patient's blood belongs to. The test relies on antigen–antibody agglutination: when antibodies in a reagent bind to matching antigens on red blood cells, visible clumping occurs.

The 3 Reagents

Each patient sample is mixed with three antisera in separate wells on a test slide:

  1. Anti-A — contains antibodies against the A antigen. If the blood carries A antigens, agglutination (clumping) occurs.
  2. Anti-B — contains antibodies against the B antigen. If the blood carries B antigens, agglutination occurs.
  3. Anti-D (Anti-Rh) — contains antibodies against the D antigen (Rh factor). Agglutination means the patient is Rh-positive.

Transfusion Compatibility Basics

A recipient can only safely receive red blood cells that do not carry antigens their plasma has antibodies against. The rules:

  • Type O− is the universal red blood cell donor (no A, B, or D antigens).
  • Type AB+ is the universal recipient (no anti-A, anti-B, or anti-D antibodies).
  • Rh-negative recipients should not receive Rh-positive blood.
  • Type A recipients can receive A and O. Type B recipients can receive B and O.

Select a patient, then add reagents to each well and observe the agglutination reaction. Based on the pattern, determine the blood type.
